# Marcus Approves

**2031 · Nuclear Facility, Tennessee**

Marcus doesn't look at the code anymore.

Twenty years as a nuclear safety engineer, and he hasn't read a line of control system code in eighteen months. Not because he can't. Because that's not his job now.

His job is judgment.

The screen in front of him shows a specification: *"Coolant flow adjustment algorithm. Purpose: Optimize thermal regulation during peak load. Constraints: Never exceed 2% flow variance. Never reduce below safety threshold."*

Below it, a summary: *"AI-generated implementation. 847 lines. All safety constraints verified. 12,000 simulation runs. Zero failures."*

Marcus reads the spec. He understands the intent, the boundaries, the failure modes. He doesn't need to understand the implementation—the AI handles that. He needs to understand whether this *should* happen.

He thinks about edge cases. Asks the system three questions. Gets answers with citations to safety protocols.

Then he taps: **Approve.**

Somewhere, code deploys. Coolant flows adjust. The reactor hums a fraction more efficiently.

Marcus walks to the window, coffee in hand. Outside, the Tennessee hills roll green under morning sun. His grandfather ran these systems with manual gauges and gut instinct. Marcus runs them with intent and approval.

The code is the AI's job. The judgment is his.

---

*The human role shifts from operator to approver.*
